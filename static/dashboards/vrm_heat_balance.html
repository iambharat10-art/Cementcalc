[Guide] What Is This Dashboard?

Imagine a giant machine that crushes rocks into powder. That's a Vertical Roller Mill (VRM). This dashboard calculates how much heat (energy) and material (stuff) goes into and comes out of that machine.

Think of it like baking cookies: you put in flour, sugar, butter (inputs), the oven adds heat, and you get cookies (output). Some heat escapes through the oven door. This dashboard tracks ALL of that — but for a cement factory.

[Play] You can change ANY number on the left sidebar and instantly see how it affects everything. Try it! Change the temperature, moisture, or production rate and watch all the numbers update.

[Factory] Three Types of Mills

[RM] Raw Mill — Crushing Rocks

Takes limestone and clay (wet rocks from the quarry) and grinds them into a fine powder called "raw meal." Hot gas from the cement kiln (a huge oven, 260°C) blows through the mill to dry the wet rocks while grinding them. This is where most of the heat energy goes — evaporating water from the rocks.

  • Gas source: Hot kiln exhaust gas (260°C, 347,000 Nm³/h)
  • Feed: 75% limestone + 25% clay, ~1.5% moisture
  • Product: 400 t/h of dry raw meal
  • Special feature: Bypass mode — gas can skip the mill and go straight to the filter
  • Optional: Cyclone separator between mill and filter

[CM] Coal Mill — Grinding Fuel

Grinds coal or petcoke into fine powder that gets blown into the kiln as fuel. This mill is dangerous because coal dust can explode! So it uses recirculating gas (63%) to keep oxygen levels low and temperatures safe. The gas goes around and around in a loop.

  • Gas source: Kiln gas at lower temperature (160°C)
  • Feed: Petcoke or coal, 1-10% moisture
  • Product: 19-48 t/h of coal powder
  • Special: 63% of exhaust gas is recycled back to the mill inlet
  • No cyclone — gas goes directly to filter
  • 25% reject — coarse particles returned to mill

[CS] Cement Mill — Making Cement

Grinds hot clinker (the cooked rocks from the kiln, ~112°C) with gypsum and limestone to make cement. There's no kiln gas here — the heat comes from the hot clinker itself! Fresh air is drawn in, and 82% of it recirculates to manage temperature. The mill outlet must stay below 90°C or the gypsum will lose its water and the cement quality suffers.

  • Gas source: Fresh air only (31,000 Nm³/h)
  • Feed: 90% clinker + 5% gypsum + 5% limestone, clinker at 112°C
  • Product: 260 t/h of cement
  • Special: 82% gas recirculation, 4,830 kg/h injection water for cooling
  • No cyclone, no bypass, no kiln gas

[Heat] Heat Balance — Where Does the Energy Go?

Everything that goes IN must come OUT. That's the law of physics (conservation of energy). This dashboard tracks every bit of heat:

HEAT INPUTS (energy going in):

  • Hot Gas — The biggest input for raw and coal mills. Hot gas from the kiln carries enormous amounts of heat. Think of it like a giant hair dryer blowing through the mill.
  • Mill Motor Power — The electric motor that turns the grinding rollers. ALL the electricity turns into heat (friction, crushing). Calculated as: Product Rate × Specific Power × 3.6 / 1000
  • Material Heat — If the feed is hot (like clinker at 112°C), it brings heat in. Cold limestone at 36°C brings very little.
  • Injection Water — Warm water sprayed into the mill (for bed stability). Even at 30°C, it carries some heat.
  • False Air — Outside air that leaks into the system through gaps, doors, seals. It's at ambient temperature.

HEAT OUTPUTS (energy going out):

  • Water Evaporation — The biggest energy consumer! Turning liquid water into steam takes a LOT of energy: 2,256 kJ per kg of water. This is why wet feed requires so much heat.
  • Stack Gas — The exhaust gas leaving through the chimney is still warm. That warmth is lost energy.
  • Radiation — Heat lost through the mill's metal walls to the surrounding air.
TOTAL HEAT IN = TOTAL HEAT OUT (always!)

Specific Heat Requirement = Total Heat In ÷ Product Rate × 1000
(tells you how much energy each kilogram of product needs)

⚖ Mass Balance — Where Does the Material Go?

Everything that goes in must come out as something. Rocks go in, powder comes out, water evaporates away.

Feed Rate = Product Rate (dry) ÷ (1 − Feed Moisture%/100)

Example: You want 400 t/h of dry product, feed moisture is 1.5%
Feed Rate = 400 ÷ (1 − 0.015) = 400 ÷ 0.985 = 406.1 t/h

Evaporation Water = Water in Feed − Water in Product + Injection Water
= (406.1 × 0.015) − (400 × 0) + (2880/1000)
= 6.09 − 0 + 2.88 = 8.97 t/h

[Wind] Gas Balance — How Much Air Flows Through?

Gas volumes are measured at two conditions:

  • Normal (Nm³/h) — Volume at 0°C and 1013.25 mbar. This is the "standard" measurement.
  • Actual (m³/h) — Volume at the real temperature and pressure. Hot gas expands, so actual volume is always bigger.
Actual Volume = Normal Volume × (273.15 + Temperature) / 273.15 × 1013.25 / Local Pressure

Example: 347,000 Nm³/h at 260°C and 983.6 mbar
= 347,000 × (273.15+260)/273.15 × 1013.25/983.6
= 347,000 × 1.952 × 1.030 = 697,740 m³/h

Barometric Pressure

The higher above sea level you are, the lower the air pressure. This matters because it affects gas density and volume.

P_atm = 1013.25 × (1 − 6.5 × Height / 288,000)^5.256

At 250m above sea level: P = 1013.25 × 0.9706 = 983.6 mbar

False Air

No system is perfectly sealed. Air leaks in through doors, maintenance hatches, and seals. We estimate this as a percentage of the total gas volume at the mill outlet. For example, "FA Mill 7%" means 7% of the mill outlet gas volume is air that leaked into the mill.

Dew Point

The temperature at which water vapor in the gas starts to condense into liquid. If the gas temperature drops below the dew point inside ducts, water droplets form and cause corrosion. We must NEVER let this happen!

⟳ Fan Sizing — How Powerful Must the Fan Be?

The fan must push ALL the gas through the entire system. It needs to overcome the pressure drop of every piece of equipment (mill, filter, ducts).

Fan Power = Actual Volume × Gas Density × ΔP × 100 / (Efficiency × 3600)

Operation: Normal running conditions
Layout: Adds safety margins (+21% on ΔP, +15% on flow) for worst-case/startup. This is the size the fan manufacturer builds.

[Tip] Best Experiments to Try:

  • Change feed moisture from 1% to 5% — watch evaporation heat skyrocket and gas volumes increase
  • Change ambient temperature from 36°C to 0°C — see how false air cools the system
  • Change injection water from 0 to 5000 kg/h — direct temperature control tool
  • Change elevation from 0 to 3000m — watch barometric pressure drop and gas volumes increase
  • Change recirculation % in coal mill — see how it affects inlet temperature
  • On cement mill: Try reducing injection water to 0 — see how outlet temp rises above 90°C (bad for gypsum!)
